Scoring rule (applies to every component)
For each answer, score 0–5 for the targeted component using this logic:
You do NOT deduct for not mentioning an unasked special case (apex, terminal velocity, etc.).
You DO deduct for incorrect statements relevant to the asked scenario, or for missing required elements the question explicitly requested.
Think of it as: did they answer the asked thing correctly, using the correct concept?

Rubric (0–5) per component
A) Velocity vs Acceleration (0–5)
Use this when the question asks about: velocity direction/magnitude, acceleration direction/magnitude, relationship between them, “speeding up vs slowing down,” or “what causes acceleration.”
5 — Correctly distinguishes velocity vs acceleration and answers the asked property (direction/magnitude/change) correctly for the scenario; no contradictory statements.
4 — Mostly correct; minor imprecision (wording, sign convention, or missing one explicitly requested detail), but the concept distinction is intact.
3 — Partial: answers some requested parts correctly, but mixes terms once or has one unclear/unsupported claim.
2 — Major conceptual error: treats velocity and acceleration as the same thing, or uses “force points where velocity points” logic, causing an incorrect answer to the asked question.
1 — Mostly wrong; shows no reliable distinction; answer is largely guesswork.
0 — No attempt / irrelevant.
Common “major error” triggers (don’t require apex):
Says acceleration is the same as velocity or always points in velocity direction.
Claims “if velocity is zero then acceleration must be zero” (unless explicitly justified by forces in that scenario).

B) Net force components → acceleration components (0–5)
Use this when the question asks about: (\sum F_x), (\sum F_y), net force direction, what forces contribute in x vs y, or resulting (a_x, a_y).
5 — Correctly uses component-wise reasoning ((\sum F_x \rightarrow a_x), (\sum F_y \rightarrow a_y)); identifies which forces act and maps them to axes correctly for the scenario asked.
4 — Correct structure but minor slip (e.g., sign convention, missing one explicitly requested force) without changing the final meaning.
3 — Some correct axis reasoning, but incomplete or contains one scalar/vector mix that creates ambiguity (still partially usable).
2 — Major error: treats forces as scalars (“(F_x+F_y=ma)” with no vector meaning), adds magnitudes regardless of direction, or assigns a force to the wrong axis in a way that breaks the conclusion.
1 — Mostly incorrect; no net-force logic.
0 — No attempt / irrelevant.
Major error triggers (scenario-agnostic):
Adds component magnitudes without direction.
Uses gravity to create horizontal acceleration in a context where gravity is the only force mentioned.
Confuses “sum of forces” with “sum of accelerations.”

C) Vector components (decomposition + interpretation) (0–5)
Use this when the question asks about: breaking a vector into x/y, comparing components, directions of components, or predicting how x/y quantities change.
5 — Correctly decomposes and/or interprets components as requested (qualitative or quantitative); uses consistent direction/sign language; answer matches scenario and what was asked.
4 — Mostly correct; small math/notation slip OR one missing requested detail; overall interpretation remains correct.
3 — Understands there are components and gives a partially correct statement, but can’t fully justify or misses a key requested part.
2 — Major error: confuses component with magnitude, swaps axes/directions, or treats components as unrelated “extra vectors,” producing an incorrect conclusion.
1 — Minimal understanding; mostly incorrect.
0 — No attempt / irrelevant.
Major error triggers:
“(v_x) is the speed” / component = magnitude confusion.
Claims components can be summed as magnitudes without direction.

D) Air resistance force (0–5)
Use this when the question asks about: direction of drag, effect of drag on motion/range/time, whether acceleration is constant with drag, how x/y behavior changes when drag is on.
5 — Correctly states drag acts opposite motion (or opposite velocity) and answers the asked effect(s) correctly for the scenario; no contradictory claims.
4 — Correct direction + at least one correct requested effect; missing a second requested effect or minor imprecision about dependence on speed.
3 — Gets general idea (“slows it down”) but weak/unclear on the specific asked effect or axis impact.
2 — Major error: wrong drag direction for the described motion, treats drag as constant in a way that breaks the asked conclusion, or claims drag increases speed/range.
1 — Mostly wrong; vague and incorrect.
0 — No attempt / irrelevant.
Major error triggers:
Drag points in a fixed global direction regardless of motion.
Claims acceleration must remain constant even when drag is included (unless the problem explicitly defines a constant resistive force model).
